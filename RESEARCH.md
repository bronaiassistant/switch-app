## Core Research Insights

### Meaningful conversation = measurable happiness
People who engage in more substantial conversations report higher wellbeing.
Asking deeper questions leads to greater bonding than small talk.

### Down Syndrome — Communication Profile
- Receptive (listening) > Expressive (speaking) — understand more than they can articulate
- Strong nonverbal communication — gestures, facial expressions, reading body language
- Short-term auditory memory challenges — keep prompts to ONE idea at a time
- Social motivation is typically HIGH — want connection but lack structured support
- Speech clarity variable — normalise pauses, never rush
- Learn best through pictures and repetition

### DS Design Principles (validated by research)
1. One task/question at a time — no multi-part questions
2. Plain language — no idioms, no jargon, short sentences
3. Icon + text pairing — never icon alone
4. Large touch targets — minimum 48px
5. High contrast text
6. Audio/read-aloud — receptive > expressive
7. No time pressure — no countdowns, timers, or auto-advance
8. Easy skip — no one should feel forced to answer
9. Consistent layouts — predictable navigation reduces cognitive load
10. No complex login — zero barrier to entry
11. Positive reinforcement — optional gentle celebration
12. Caregiver/carer setup mode — accessible but not patronising
13. Turn-taking prompts — "let's go around" style
14. Concrete, familiar topics — avoid abstract hypotheticals
15. Age-respectful tone — adults with DS are adults

### Neurodiversity general principles
- W3C Cognitive Accessibility guidelines
- Design for the edges, benefit everyone (Kerb Cut effect)
- Breathing/calming tools reduce anxiety barrier
- Read-aloud supports dyslexia, vision needs, DS
- Topic filters = safe spaces
- No accounts = lowest barrier

### Competitive Landscape
- Yakable — restaurant metaphor, structured but complex
- Party Qs — volume, fun, ad-supported
- Big Talk — depth, movement brand
- Banter — AI practice mode
- Presence — family/friends focus
- NONE address DS or neurodivergent users specifically

## The Mission (from research)

> "Imagine a scenario: a young adult with Down syndrome is out with coworkers and uses your app to suggest a fun question; the group engages, laughs, and in the process, that young adult gets to participate more fully than they might have otherwise."

This is the product vision. Not a therapy tool. Not a special needs app. A conversation tool that works for everyone — and happens to work especially well for people who are often left out.

## The Bridge Metaphor (final vision note)
> "Over time, users learn from the app’s prompts and the social response they get — gradually needing the app less as they gain confidence initiating conversations on their own."

This is the long-term product vision: Switch as a bridge, not a crutch. Used less over time as confidence grows. That’s the outcome worth building for.

## Privacy & Ethics Notes
- People with DS may have limited awareness of privacy risks online
- App should collect ZERO personal data
- Any sharing features need clear, simple consent language
- Caregiver vetting is common — be transparent about what the app does
- No accounts = no data = highest trust
